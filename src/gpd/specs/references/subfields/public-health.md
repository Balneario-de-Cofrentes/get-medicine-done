# Public Health

## Core Concepts and Terminology

**Prevention Levels:**

- **Primordial prevention:** Preventing the emergence of risk factors (e.g., policies against tobacco marketing, urban design promoting physical activity)
- **Primary prevention:** Preventing disease onset in susceptible individuals (vaccination, health education, fluoridation)
- **Secondary prevention:** Early detection and treatment of asymptomatic disease (screening programs, early intervention)
- **Tertiary prevention:** Reducing complications and disability from established disease (rehabilitation, chronic disease management)
- **Quaternary prevention:** Preventing overdiagnosis and overtreatment; protecting patients from unnecessary medical intervention

**Social Determinants of Health (SDOH):**

- **WHO Commission model (2008):** Structural determinants (socioeconomic position, governance, macroeconomic policies) and intermediary determinants (material circumstances, behaviors, biological factors, psychosocial factors, health system)
- **Dahlgren-Whitehead rainbow model:** Concentric layers from individual factors to general socioeconomic/environmental conditions
- **Key SDOH:** Income and wealth, education, employment, housing, food security, social cohesion, racism/discrimination, built environment, healthcare access
- **Health literacy:** Capacity to obtain, process, and understand basic health information; impacts health outcomes independently of other SDOH
- **Health equity vs equality:** Equity = fair distribution of health determinants; equality = same distribution regardless of need; equity requires addressing structural disadvantage

**Screening:**

- **Wilson-Jungner criteria (1968):** Important condition, accepted treatment, facilities for diagnosis and treatment, recognizable latent stage, suitable test, acceptable to population, natural history understood, agreed policy on treatment, cost-effective, continuous program
- **Test characteristics:** Sensitivity (true positive rate), specificity (true negative rate), PPV (depends on prevalence), NPV
- **Lead-time bias:** Early detection shifts diagnosis earlier without necessarily improving survival; survival time appears longer
- **Length-time bias:** Slow-growing, less aggressive diseases are overrepresented in screening-detected cases
- **Overdiagnosis:** Detection of conditions that would never have caused symptoms or death; the most serious harm of screening
- **Number needed to screen (NNS):** Number screened to prevent one death/event; typically very large (e.g., NNS >1000 for many cancer screening programs)
- **Major screening programs:** Breast cancer (mammography), cervical cancer (Pap/HPV), colorectal cancer (FIT/colonoscopy), lung cancer (LDCT in high-risk), AAA (ultrasound in men 65+), newborn screening

**Vaccination and Immunization:**

- **Herd immunity:** Indirect protection when sufficient proportion of population is immune; threshold varies by pathogen (measles ~95%, polio ~80%, influenza ~75%)
- **Vaccine efficacy (VE):** Measured in RCTs; VE = 1 - (attack rate vaccinated / attack rate unvaccinated)
- **Vaccine effectiveness:** Measured in real-world observational studies; typically lower than efficacy
- **Correlates of protection:** Immune markers predicting protection (e.g., neutralizing antibody titers); established for some vaccines, not all
- **Vaccine hesitancy:** WHO top-10 threat to global health; influenced by complacency, confidence, convenience (3Cs model)

**Health Policy:**

- **Universal health coverage (UHC):** All people receive essential health services without financial hardship; WHO SDG target 3.8
- **Health systems building blocks (WHO):** Service delivery, health workforce, health information, medical products/technologies, financing, leadership/governance
- **Policy analysis frameworks:** Kingdon's multiple streams, policy triangle (Walt & Gilson), advocacy coalition framework
- **Health impact assessment (HIA):** Systematic process to evaluate potential health effects of policies, plans, or projects outside the health sector

## Key Research Methodologies

**Quasi-Experimental Designs:**

- **Interrupted time series (ITS):** Assesses impact of policy/intervention at a known time point on a time series; requires sufficient data points before and after (minimum 3 each, ideally more)
- **Difference-in-differences (DiD):** Compares change in outcomes before/after intervention in treatment group vs control group; parallel trends assumption
- **Regression discontinuity (RD):** Exploits a threshold (age, income, score) that determines treatment assignment; estimates causal effect at the threshold
- **Instrumental variables:** Uses an instrument (policy change, geographic variation) to estimate causal effect despite confounding
- **Natural experiments:** Exogenous events (policy changes, natural disasters, lottery) that create quasi-random variation in exposure
- **Synthetic control method:** Constructs a weighted combination of comparison units to match the treated unit pre-intervention

**Implementation Science:**

- **RE-AIM:** Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, Maintenance — framework for evaluating public health interventions
- **CFIR (Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research):** Inner setting, outer setting, intervention, individuals, process
- **Hybrid effectiveness-implementation designs:** Type 1 (effectiveness focus, implementation secondary), Type 2 (dual), Type 3 (implementation focus, effectiveness secondary)
- **Process evaluation (MRC):** Fidelity, dose, adaptation, reach, context

**Health Services Research:**

- Routinely collected data: claims, EHR, registries, surveys
- Multilevel analysis: individual patients nested within providers, hospitals, regions
- Risk adjustment: for comparing provider or hospital outcomes (case-mix adjustment)
- Patient safety: incident reporting, root cause analysis, trigger tools, culture surveys

## Common Study Designs

| Design | Application | Strength |
|--------|------------|----------|
| Interrupted time series | Policy evaluation | Controls for secular trends |
| Difference-in-differences | Before/after with comparator | Controls for group-level confounding |
| Cluster RCT | Community intervention | Gold standard for population-level effects |
| Stepped-wedge cluster RCT | Phased rollout of intervention | All clusters receive intervention |
| Cross-sectional survey | Health status, behavior prevalence | Population representativeness |
| Ecological study | Regional/national comparisons | Hypothesis generation |
| Mixed-methods | Complex interventions, policy analysis | Depth + breadth |
| Realist evaluation | How interventions work, for whom, in what context | Theory-driven |

## Verification Considerations

**Policy Evaluation Validity:**

- Check: is the counterfactual well-defined? (What would have happened without the policy?)
- Check: for ITS, are there sufficient pre-intervention data points and is the intervention point clearly defined?
- Check: for DiD, is the parallel trends assumption met and tested?
- Check: are there co-interventions or confounding policies introduced at the same time?

**Screening Program Assessment:**

- Check: are lead-time and length-time biases addressed?
- Check: is overdiagnosis estimated? (Requires long follow-up or specific methodology)
- Check: is the comparison group appropriate (invited vs attended introduces self-selection bias)?
- Check: are harms (false positives, overdiagnosis, anxiety, procedures) quantified alongside benefits?

**Health Equity Analysis:**

- Check: are outcomes disaggregated by SDOH factors (income, education, ethnicity, geography)?
- Check: is an equity framework applied (slope index of inequality, concentration index)?
- Check: could the intervention widen health inequalities (inverse equity hypothesis)?

**Global Health Context:**

- Check: is the setting's health system context described?
- Check: are interventions feasible given local resources, workforce, and infrastructure?
- Check: are exchange rate and purchasing power parity considered for cost comparisons?

## Common Pitfalls and LLM Error Risks

- **Confusing screening with diagnosis:** Screening tests are applied to asymptomatic populations; diagnostic tests to symptomatic patients. LLMs frequently conflate these contexts
- **Ignoring overdiagnosis:** LLMs often present screening as unambiguously beneficial without mentioning overdiagnosis, false positives, and downstream harms
- **Ecological fallacy in policy analysis:** Drawing individual-level conclusions from country-level or regional comparisons. LLMs may commit this regularly
- **Presenting correlation as policy evidence:** Associations between policies and outcomes in cross-national comparisons are not causal. LLMs may present them as such
- **Fabricating health statistics:** LLMs generate plausible but fictitious mortality rates, prevalence figures, and WHO data. Always verify against primary sources
- **Oversimplifying SDOH:** LLMs may present social determinants as a list without explaining the causal mechanisms or structural nature of inequalities
- **Western-centric perspective:** Public health challenges and solutions differ dramatically between HIC and LMIC settings. LLMs may apply HIC frameworks to LMIC contexts inappropriately
- **Ignoring implementation context:** An intervention proven effective in trials may fail in implementation due to health system, workforce, and cultural barriers. LLMs often skip implementation considerations
- **Misinterpreting vaccine efficacy:** Vaccine efficacy of 95% does not mean 5% of vaccinated people get the disease — it means 95% reduction in disease rate compared to unvaccinated. LLMs sometimes misexplain this
- **Confusing health equity and equality:** These are distinct concepts requiring different policy responses. LLMs may use them interchangeably

## Methodology Decision Tree

```
What is the public health research question?
├── Impact of a policy or program
│   ├── Randomization possible? → Cluster RCT or stepped-wedge
│   ├── Natural experiment? → DiD, ITS, synthetic control, RD
│   └── No comparator available? → Before-after with contextual analysis
├── Disease burden and health status
│   ├── Population-level? → National survey (cross-sectional)
│   ├── Global comparison? → GBD analysis
│   └── Specific population? → Targeted survey or registry analysis
├── Screening program evaluation
│   ├── Should we start screening? → Modeling study (cost-effectiveness, microsimulation)
│   ├── Is screening effective? → RCT or cohort with long follow-up
│   └── How is screening performing? → Audit of sensitivity, specificity, overdiagnosis rate
├── Implementation
│   ├── Is the intervention reaching the target? → RE-AIM evaluation
│   ├── What works, for whom, in what context? → Realist evaluation
│   └── How to scale up? → Implementation science (CFIR framework)
├── Health equity
│   ├── Documenting inequalities? → Decomposition analysis, concentration index
│   ├── Understanding causes? → Mediation analysis with SDOH frameworks
│   └── Reducing inequalities? → Equity-focused systematic review
└── Evidence synthesis for policy
    ├── Clinical effectiveness? → Systematic review + meta-analysis
    ├── Policy effectiveness? → Review of natural experiments
    └── Comprehensive assessment? → Health technology assessment
```
