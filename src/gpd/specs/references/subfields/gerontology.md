# Gerontology

## Core Concepts and Terminology

**Aging Biology:**

- **Hallmarks of aging (Lopez-Otin 2023):** Genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, loss of proteostasis, disabled macroautophagy, deregulated nutrient sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, stem cell exhaustion, altered intercellular communication, chronic inflammation, dysbiosis
- **Cellular senescence:** Irreversible cell cycle arrest; senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) drives chronic inflammation; senolytics (dasatinib + quercetin, fisetin) are under investigation
- **Inflammaging:** Chronic low-grade inflammation associated with aging; elevated IL-6, TNF-alpha, CRP; contributes to multimorbidity
- **Immunosenescence:** Age-related decline in immune function; reduced naive T-cells, thymic involution, impaired vaccine response
- **Epigenetic clocks:** DNA methylation-based biological age estimators; Horvath clock, Hannum clock, GrimAge, PhenoAge, DunedinPACE (pace of aging)
- **Telomere biology:** Progressive shortening with cell division; critically short telomeres trigger senescence; leukocyte telomere length as biomarker (imperfect)

**Frailty:**

- **Fried phenotype model (CHS criteria):** 3+ of: unintentional weight loss, self-reported exhaustion, low physical activity, slow walking speed, weak grip strength; 1-2 = pre-frail
- **Rockwood deficit accumulation model (Frailty Index):** Ratio of accumulated health deficits to total deficits assessed; continuous variable (0-1); >0.25 typically frail
- **Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS):** 9-point clinical judgment scale; widely used in acute care and pandemic triage
- **FRAIL scale:** Fatigue, Resistance, Ambulation, Illnesses, Loss of weight — 5-item screening tool
- **Intrinsic capacity (WHO):** Composite of locomotion, vitality, cognition, psychological, sensory capacities; functional ability = intrinsic capacity + environment

**Sarcopenia:**

- **EWGSOP2 definition (2019):** Low muscle strength (grip or chair stand) + low muscle quantity/quality; severity defined by low physical performance
- **Diagnosis:** DXA for appendicular skeletal muscle mass (ASM); BIA as alternative; grip strength by dynamometry; gait speed, SPPB, TUG for physical performance
- **Cut-offs:** ASM/height2 <7.0 kg/m2 (men), <5.5 kg/m2 (women) by DXA; grip <27 kg (men), <16 kg (women); gait speed <0.8 m/s

**Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA):**

- Multidimensional, systematic assessment of older adults covering: medical, functional, cognitive, psychological, social, nutritional, environmental domains
- Instruments: ADL (Katz), IADL (Lawton), MMSE/MoCA (cognition), GDS (depression), MNA (nutrition), Tinetti/Berg (balance), Get Up and Go
- CGA-guided interventions reduce mortality, institutionalization, and functional decline (strong evidence)

**Multimorbidity and Polypharmacy:**

- **Multimorbidity:** Co-occurrence of >=2 chronic conditions; the norm in older adults (>65% of those aged 65+)
- **Polypharmacy:** Commonly defined as >=5 regular medications; hyperpolypharmacy >=10
- **Prescribing cascades:** Side effect of drug A misinterpreted as new condition, treated with drug B
- **Deprescribing:** Systematic, supervised withdrawal of inappropriate medications; use STOPP/START criteria, Beers criteria, or MedStopper

## Key Research Methodologies

**Longitudinal Cohort Studies:**

- Essential for studying aging trajectories (linear, accelerated, resilient)
- Major cohorts: Framingham (cardiovascular), Whitehall II (social determinants), SHARE (European aging), Health and Retirement Study (HRS), English Longitudinal Study of Ageing (ELSA)
- Challenges: survivor bias (only healthy survive to be studied), selective attrition, period and cohort effects
- Analysis: growth curve models, latent class trajectory analysis, joint models for longitudinal and survival data

**Intervention Studies in Older Adults:**

- Recruitment challenges: comorbidities, cognitive impairment, transportation, polypharmacy
- Eligibility: overly restrictive criteria exclude typical geriatric patients; pragmatic designs preferred
- Endpoints: composite outcomes (death + hospitalization + functional decline), patient-reported outcomes, goal attainment scaling
- Safety: falls, delirium, medication-related adverse events require specific monitoring
- CGA as intervention: cluster-randomized trials of CGA vs usual care

**Biomarker Studies:**

- Epigenetic clocks: residual (biological age - chronological age) predicts mortality and morbidity
- Frailty biomarkers: GDF-15, IL-6, CRP, albumin, cystatin C — panels outperform single markers
- Imaging biomarkers: brain MRI (white matter hyperintensities, hippocampal volume), DXA (body composition), CT (muscle quality/myosteatosis)

## Common Study Designs

| Design | Application in Gerontology | Key Consideration |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Longitudinal cohort | Aging trajectories, risk factors for frailty | Survivor bias, attrition |
| Cluster RCT | CGA, geriatric care models, deprescribing | ICC for power calculation |
| Stepped-wedge | Service delivery interventions | All clusters eventually receive intervention |
| Cross-sectional | Prevalence of frailty, sarcopenia | Cannot distinguish age from cohort effects |
| N-of-1 trial | Individualized medication management | Ideal for polypharmacy decisions |
| Before-after | Quality improvement in geriatric units | No control, regression to mean |

## Verification Considerations

**Frailty Assessment Validity:**

- Check: which frailty model was used (phenotype, index, CFS)? They do not produce interchangeable results
- Check: are cut-points validated for the specific population (different for community vs hospital, different ethnic groups)?
- Check: is pre-frailty reported separately from frailty?

**Age-Related Confounding:**

- Check: is chronological age adequately controlled? Non-linear age effects may require splines or polynomial terms
- Check: is survivorship bias considered? Studies of very old adults inherently study survivors
- Check: are cohort effects distinguished from age effects? Cross-sectional studies cannot

**Outcome Measure Appropriateness:**

- Check: are outcomes patient-centered (function, quality of life, independence) rather than purely disease-specific?
- Check: is mortality a competing risk for non-fatal outcomes? (standard KM and Cox handle this poorly)
- Check: are ceiling/floor effects in functional scales addressed?

**Medication-Related:**

- Check: is polypharmacy quantified and accounted for as confounder or effect modifier?
- Check: are potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs) assessed using validated criteria?
- Check: is prescribing cascading identified?

## Common Pitfalls and LLM Error Risks

- **Conflating biological and chronological age:** LLMs may use "old" to mean chronologically old without considering biological heterogeneity at any given age
- **Treating frailty as binary:** Frailty is a spectrum; the Frailty Index is continuous. LLMs may oversimplify frailty as present/absent
- **Ignoring competing risks:** In elderly populations, death competes with non-fatal outcomes. Standard Kaplan-Meier overestimates cumulative incidence of events when mortality is high. LLMs rarely mention competing risk analysis
- **Extrapolating from younger populations:** Drug trials typically exclude older adults with multimorbidity. LLMs may apply efficacy data from younger populations without caveating this limitation
- **Conflating dementia types:** Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, and frontotemporal dementia have different pathophysiology. LLMs may treat "dementia" as a single entity
- **Fabricating longevity statistics:** LLMs may generate fictitious life expectancy data or survival statistics. Always verify against WHO, UN, or national statistical agencies
- **Misrepresenting CGA evidence:** CGA is not a screening tool; it is a management approach. LLMs may describe it incorrectly
- **Confusing EWGSOP and EWGSOP2:** The 2019 update (EWGSOP2) changed the diagnostic algorithm, making low strength (not low mass) the primary criterion. LLMs may cite the older algorithm
- **Ageism in language:** LLMs may use ageist language ("elderly" as a noun, "senile") that is no longer acceptable in geriatric medicine. Preferred: "older adults," "people aged 65+"

## Methodology Decision Tree

```
What is the gerontology research question?
├── Prevalence/burden of frailty or geriatric syndromes
│   ├── Population-based? → Cross-sectional survey with validated instruments
│   └── Hospital setting? → Point prevalence study in admitted patients
├── Risk factors for functional decline
│   ├── Modifiable factors? → Prospective cohort with repeated measures
│   └── Genetic/epigenetic? → GWAS, epigenetic clock analysis
├── Intervention to prevent/reverse frailty
│   ├── Exercise? → RCT with multicomponent intervention
│   ├── Nutrition? → RCT with protein supplementation, MNA-guided
│   ├── Deprescribing? → Cluster RCT or stepped-wedge
│   └── CGA-based care? → Cluster RCT comparing CGA to usual care
├── Biological aging mechanism
│   ├── Senescence? → Translational study, senolytic trial
│   └── Biomarker discovery? → Nested case-control within longitudinal cohort
└── Health services / care models
    ├── Geriatric unit effectiveness? → Before-after or stepped-wedge
    └── Cost-effectiveness? → Economic evaluation alongside trial
```
