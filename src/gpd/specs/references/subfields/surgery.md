# Surgery

## Core Concepts and Terminology

**Surgical Classification:**

- **By urgency:** Elective (planned), urgent (within hours-days), emergency (immediate)
- **By purpose:** Diagnostic (biopsy, exploration), curative (resection, repair), palliative (symptom relief), cosmetic
- **By invasiveness:** Open, minimally invasive surgery (MIS) — laparoscopic, thoracoscopic; robotic-assisted (da Vinci); single-incision laparoscopic surgery (SILS); natural orifice transluminal endoscopic surgery (NOTES)
- **By specialty:** General surgery, orthopedic, cardiac, thoracic, vascular, neurosurgery, urology, gynecology, otolaryngology, ophthalmology, plastic/reconstructive, transplant

**Perioperative Care:**

- **Preoperative assessment:** ASA classification (I-VI), functional capacity (METs), cardiac risk assessment (Revised Cardiac Risk Index / Lee Index), pulmonary risk (ARISCAT), nutritional status, frailty assessment
- **Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS):** Multimodal evidence-based perioperative care pathways; 20+ elements including preoperative counseling, carbohydrate loading, minimizing fasting, multimodal analgesia, early mobilization, early enteral nutrition
- **Surgical site infection (SSI) prevention:** Antimicrobial prophylaxis (within 60 minutes of incision), skin preparation (chlorhexidine-alcohol), normothermia, glycemic control, wound classification (clean, clean-contaminated, contaminated, dirty)
- **Venous thromboembolism (VTE) prophylaxis:** Risk assessment (Caprini score), mechanical (compression stockings, intermittent pneumatic compression), pharmacological (LMWH, UFH, DOACs)
- **Postoperative complications:** Clavien-Dindo classification (Grade I-V; I=deviation from normal, V=death); Comprehensive Complication Index (CCI) — 0-100 scale
- **30-day and 90-day mortality:** Standard timeframes for reporting surgical mortality; 90-day increasingly preferred for cancer surgery

**Surgical Outcomes:**

- **Procedure-specific metrics:** Operative time, blood loss, conversion rate (MIS to open), length of stay, readmission rate, reoperation rate
- **Quality indicators:** Volume-outcome relationship (high-volume centers have better outcomes for complex surgery), surgeon case volume, textbook outcome (composite of ideal recovery)
- **Patient-reported outcomes after surgery:** EORTC QLQ (cancer surgery), Oxford scores (joint replacement), EQ-5D, PROMIS
- **Failure to rescue (FTR):** Death after a major complication; reflects quality of complication management, not just complication prevention

**Minimally Invasive Surgery:**

- **Laparoscopic advantages:** Reduced pain, shorter hospital stay, faster recovery, fewer wound complications, better cosmesis
- **Laparoscopic risks:** Trocar injuries, pneumoperitoneum effects (CO2 absorption, diaphragm pressure), longer operative times (learning curve), limited tactile feedback
- **Robotic surgery:** Enhanced 3D visualization, wristed instruments, tremor filtration; higher equipment cost; da Vinci system (Intuitive Surgical) dominates
- **Learning curve:** CUSUM analysis to assess proficiency; typically 20-50 cases for basic laparoscopy, 50-200 for complex procedures, variable for robotic

**Surgical Oncology Principles:**

- **Margin status:** R0 (microscopic negative), R1 (microscopic positive), R2 (macroscopic residual); R0 is the goal of curative surgery
- **Lymph node dissection:** Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) vs systematic lymphadenectomy; minimum node counts vary by cancer
- **Neoadjuvant therapy:** Pre-surgical treatment (chemo, radiation, immunotherapy) to downstage tumor, assess response, and enable surgery
- **Pathological complete response (pCR):** No viable tumor in resected specimen after neoadjuvant treatment; prognostic surrogate

## Key Research Methodologies

**Surgical Trial Design:**

- Blinding challenges: sham surgery is ethically complex and rarely used (ORBITA, CSAW trials are notable exceptions)
- Expertise-based trial design: randomize to surgeon/team rather than procedure; avoids learning curve effects
- Pragmatic surgical trials: broad eligibility, routine practice settings
- Pilot/feasibility studies: essential before definitive surgical RCTs; assess recruitment, randomization acceptance, intervention delivery
- Standardization: operative technique must be standardized and learning curve addressed before definitive trial
- Equipoise: genuine uncertainty about which intervention is better; essential for ethical randomization

**Surgical Registries:**

- National surgical quality databases: ACS-NSQIP (US), National Bowel Cancer Audit (UK), Swedish Quality Registries
- Risk adjustment: O/E ratios (observed/expected outcomes) accounting for case mix
- Funnel plots: comparing institutional outcomes with control limits
- Registry-based RCTs: leverage existing registries for efficient trial conduct (TASTE, SORT OUT trials)

**Learning Curve Analysis:**

- CUSUM (cumulative sum): sequential monitoring of outcomes; detects when performance changes
- CUSUM of operative time: time decreases as proficiency increases
- Risk-adjusted CUSUM (RA-CUSUM): accounts for case difficulty
- Minimum case volume: regulatory and credentialing requirement for complex procedures

**Quality Improvement:**

- Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycles: iterative improvement methodology
- Statistical process control: run charts, control charts to detect special cause variation
- Surgical safety checklist (WHO): pre-induction, pre-incision, pre-transfer; reduces complications and mortality

## Verification Considerations

**Surgical Trial Quality:**

- Check: was the surgical technique standardized? Is it described with sufficient detail (TIDieR)?
- Check: was the surgeon learning curve addressed (minimum case volume, CUSUM analysis)?
- Check: is the comparator a genuine alternative (not a straw man)?
- Check: was outcome assessment blinded (possible even when procedure is not)?
- Check: is ITT analysis performed (patients analyzed in their assigned group regardless of crossover)?

**Outcome Reporting:**

- Check: are complications graded using Clavien-Dindo or CCI?
- Check: is 30-day AND 90-day mortality reported for major cancer surgery?
- Check: are patient-reported outcomes included alongside surgeon-reported outcomes?
- Check: is length of stay reported alongside readmission rate (short stay with high readmission may negate benefit)?

**Comparative Effectiveness:**

- Check: is volume-outcome relationship accounted for when comparing techniques?
- Check: are conversion rates (MIS to open) reported and analyzed (ITT vs as-treated)?
- Check: is case selection bias addressed (MIS may be performed on easier cases)?

**Surgical Innovation (IDEAL Framework):**

- Check: is the innovation stage correctly identified (idea, development, exploration, assessment, long-term study)?
- Check: has the innovation progressed through appropriate stages before assessment in RCT?
- Check: is the regulatory pathway followed (CE marking, FDA clearance/approval for devices)?

## Common Pitfalls and LLM Error Risks

- **Confusing efficacy of procedure with skill of surgeon:** Surgical outcomes depend heavily on individual surgeon and institutional volume. LLMs may present average outcomes as universally applicable
- **Ignoring the learning curve:** New surgical techniques have steep learning curves. Early adopter results are worse than mature practice results. LLMs may cite early outcomes data
- **Overestimating robotic surgery benefits:** Robotic surgery has not consistently demonstrated superiority over conventional laparoscopy in RCTs for most procedures. LLMs may overstate its advantages
- **Fabricating surgical outcome data:** LLMs generate plausible but fictitious complication rates, conversion rates, and survival data. Always verify against registry data or published trials
- **Ignoring selection bias in observational surgical data:** Healthier patients undergo surgery; sicker patients receive conservative management. Comparing outcomes without risk adjustment is misleading
- **Confusing open and laparoscopic trial results:** Outcomes from open surgery trials cannot be extrapolated to laparoscopic approaches and vice versa
- **Missing the ERAS context:** Modern perioperative care (ERAS) has dramatically changed postoperative outcomes. Historical comparisons without accounting for ERAS evolution are misleading
- **Oversimplifying sham surgery ethics:** Sham surgical procedures raise unique ethical considerations (anesthesia risk, invasive placebo). LLMs may either dismiss or overstate these concerns
- **Ignoring operative mortality context:** A 2% operative mortality for a minor procedure is alarming; 5% for a Whipple procedure may be acceptable. Context matters enormously
- **Conflating volume and quality:** While volume-outcome relationships exist, they are not absolute. LLMs may present volume thresholds as rigid when they are statistical tendencies

## Methodology Decision Tree

```
What is the surgical research question?
├── Is technique A better than technique B?
│   ├── Both established? → Pragmatic RCT (expertise-based if different skill sets)
│   ├── One is new? → IDEAL framework: feasibility → pilot → RCT
│   └── Sham control justified? → Sham-controlled RCT (rare, requires ethics justification)
├── What are the outcomes of procedure X?
│   ├── Single center? → Prospective case series with standardized outcomes
│   ├── Multi-center? → Registry analysis (NSQIP, national audit)
│   └── Learning curve? → CUSUM or RA-CUSUM analysis
├── How to improve perioperative care?
│   ├── ERAS pathway? → Stepped-wedge cluster RCT or before-after with controls
│   ├── Surgical safety checklist? → Stepped-wedge or ITS
│   └── Single element (drug, device)? → Patient-level RCT
├── Volume-outcome relationship?
│   ├── → Large registry analysis with risk adjustment (propensity score, instrumental variable)
├── Innovation assessment?
│   ├── New device? → IDEAL stages + registry (1-Development, 2a-Exploration, 2b-Assessment)
│   ├── New technique? → Prospective registry → feasibility RCT → definitive RCT
│   └── Modification of existing? → Before-after or cohort within registry
└── Economic evaluation?
    ├── Alongside trial? → Trial-based CEA with resource use collection
    └── Decision model? → Markov or microsimulation with surgical pathway
```
