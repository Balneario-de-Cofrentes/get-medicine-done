# Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation

## Core Concepts and Terminology

**Functional Assessment:**

- **ICF Framework (WHO International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health):** Body functions/structures, activities, participation; contextual factors (environmental, personal)
- **Barthel Index:** 10 items measuring ADL independence (feeding, bathing, grooming, dressing, bowels, bladder, toileting, transfers, mobility, stairs); 0-100 score
- **Functional Independence Measure (FIM):** 18 items across motor (13) and cognitive (5) domains; 7-point scale per item (total 18-126); gold standard in inpatient rehabilitation
- **Modified Rankin Scale (mRS):** 0-6 scale for disability after stroke; 0=no symptoms, 6=dead; most common stroke outcome measure
- **Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS):** 0-100 scale originally for oncology; used broadly in rehabilitation
- **Berg Balance Scale:** 14 items assessing static and dynamic balance; 0-56; <45 = fall risk
- **Timed Up and Go (TUG):** Stand, walk 3m, turn, return, sit; >12-14 seconds = fall risk in elderly
- **6-Minute Walk Test (6MWT):** Submaximal exercise test; distance walked in 6 minutes; cardiopulmonary and functional capacity
- **Short Physical Performance Battery (SPPB):** Balance, gait speed, chair stand; 0-12; widely used in geriatric research

**Rehabilitation Domains:**

- **Neurological rehabilitation:** Stroke (acute, subacute, chronic phases), traumatic brain injury (TBI), spinal cord injury (SCI), multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's disease
- **Musculoskeletal rehabilitation:** Post-surgical (arthroplasty, ACL, rotator cuff), fracture, chronic pain, osteoarthritis, back pain
- **Cardiac rehabilitation:** Phase I (inpatient), Phase II (outpatient supervised), Phase III (maintenance); exercise training, education, psychosocial support
- **Pulmonary rehabilitation:** COPD, interstitial lung disease, post-COVID; exercise training + education + behavior change; minimum 8 weeks, twice weekly
- **Cancer rehabilitation (prehabilitation):** Pre-surgical conditioning, post-treatment functional recovery, fatigue management, lymphedema

**Therapeutic Modalities:**

- **Exercise therapy:** Aerobic (cardiorespiratory fitness), resistance (strength), flexibility, balance, functional training; dose prescribed as frequency, intensity, type, time (FITT)
- **Manual therapy:** Joint mobilization/manipulation, soft tissue techniques, neural mobilization
- **Electrotherapy:** TENS (pain), NMES/FES (muscle activation), ultrasound (tissue healing), interferential current, laser therapy
- **Hydrotherapy:** Aquatic exercise exploiting buoyancy, hydrostatic pressure, thermal effects; evidence strongest for osteoarthritis, fibromyalgia, low back pain
- **Robotics and technology-assisted:** Exoskeletons (Lokomat, Ekso), virtual reality, brain-computer interfaces, telerehabilitation
- **Occupational therapy:** ADL training, cognitive rehabilitation, environmental modification, assistive technology, splinting, vocational rehabilitation
- **Speech-language pathology:** Dysphagia, aphasia, dysarthria, cognitive-communication disorders

**Pain Management in Rehabilitation:**

- **Biopsychosocial model:** Pain is influenced by biological, psychological (catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, depression), and social (work, relationships) factors
- **Central sensitization:** Amplified CNS processing of nociceptive and non-nociceptive signals; fibromyalgia, chronic widespread pain
- **Graded exposure / graded motor imagery:** Progressive approach to feared movements / imagined movements before physical execution
- **Pain neuroscience education (PNE):** Teaching patients about pain mechanisms to reduce threat perception

## Key Research Methodologies

**Rehabilitation Trial Design:**

- Blinding challenges: exercise and physical interventions are difficult to blind; sham interventions (detuned ultrasound, sham TENS) used when possible
- Complex interventions framework (MRC): development, feasibility/piloting, evaluation, implementation
- Pragmatic trials preferred for service delivery questions; explanatory for mechanism
- Dose-response studies: investigating optimal frequency, intensity, duration of rehabilitation programs
- Active comparator designs: comparing two active treatments (e.g., robotics vs conventional physiotherapy) rather than treatment vs no treatment

**Outcome Measurement:**

- COSMIN (Consensus-based Standards for the selection of health Measurement Instruments): framework for evaluating measurement properties (reliability, validity, responsiveness)
- Minimal clinically important difference (MCID): smallest change patients perceive as meaningful
- Core outcome sets (COS): standardized sets of outcomes for specific conditions (COMET Initiative)
- Patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs): SF-36, EQ-5D, condition-specific measures (DASH, KOOS, ODI)
- Performance-based outcome measures: grip strength, gait speed, 6MWT, SPPB

**Implementation Science in Rehabilitation:**

- Knowledge translation: bridging research-to-practice gap
- Behaviour change techniques: COM-B model, Theoretical Domains Framework
- Process evaluation alongside trials: fidelity, dose, reach, adaptation

## Common Study Designs

| Design | Application | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| RCT with sham control | Isolating specific modality effect | Sham TENS vs active TENS |
| Pragmatic RCT | Comparing rehabilitation programs | Inpatient vs home-based rehab |
| Stepped-wedge cluster RCT | Service implementation | Rolling out new rehab pathway |
| Single-case experimental design | Individual response to intervention | ABA or ABAB design for rare conditions |
| Dose-finding study | Optimal exercise prescription | Varying frequency/intensity of training |
| Non-inferiority RCT | Telerehabilitation vs in-person | Demonstrating equivalent outcomes |
| Mixed-methods | Understanding patient experience alongside outcomes | Qualitative interviews + RCT data |

## Verification Considerations

**Intervention Description:**

- Check: is the intervention described with sufficient detail for replication? (TIDieR checklist)
- Check: are exercise parameters specified (sets, reps, intensity, frequency, duration, progression)?
- Check: is the comparator described with equal detail?
- Check: is treatment fidelity monitored and reported?

**Outcome Measure Validity:**

- Check: are outcome measures validated for the specific population and condition?
- Check: is the MCID defined and results interpreted against it?
- Check: are both patient-reported AND performance-based measures included?
- Check: are floor/ceiling effects in outcome measures acknowledged?

**Rehabilitation-Specific Biases:**

- Check: is allocation concealment adequate? (Blinding of allocation is possible even when blinding of treatment is not)
- Check: is assessor blinding maintained? (Even when participant blinding is impossible)
- Check: is attention/contact time equalized between groups? (Hawthorne effect)
- Check: is spontaneous recovery accounted for (especially in neurological conditions)?
- Check: is co-intervention controlled (patients may seek additional treatment outside the study)?

**Dose-Response Adequacy:**

- Check: is the rehabilitation dose sufficient to expect a response? (Underdosed studies give false negative results)
- Check: is adherence monitored and reported? (Low adherence dilutes treatment effect)
- Check: is the control group truly untreated or does it receive "usual care" that may include substantial rehabilitation?

## Common Pitfalls and LLM Error Risks

- **Ignoring the biopsychosocial model:** LLMs tend to focus on biomedical aspects of pain and disability, neglecting psychological and social factors that are central to rehabilitation
- **Overstating evidence for passive modalities:** Evidence for ultrasound, laser, TENS is generally weak for most conditions. LLMs may present these as established treatments
- **Confusing efficacy and effectiveness in rehabilitation:** What works in a controlled research setting may not translate to real-world clinical practice due to dose, adherence, and therapist skill variation
- **Ignoring spontaneous recovery:** In stroke and TBI, substantial recovery occurs naturally in the first 3-6 months. Studies without adequate control groups cannot attribute improvement to the intervention
- **Fabricating MCID values:** LLMs may generate plausible but incorrect MCID values for rehabilitation outcome measures. Always verify against published psychometric studies
- **Oversimplifying exercise prescription:** "Exercise is beneficial" without specifying the type, dose, progression, and population is clinically unhelpful
- **Misinterpreting the FIM:** FIM scores are ordinal, not interval; arithmetic means are statistically inappropriate (though widely used in practice). LLMs may not flag this measurement issue
- **Conflating different rehabilitation phases:** Acute, subacute, and chronic phases require different interventions and have different expected outcomes. LLMs may provide generic rehabilitation advice
- **Technology hype:** Robotic and VR-based rehabilitation has not consistently shown superiority over conventional therapy at equivalent doses. LLMs may overstate the evidence for technology-assisted rehabilitation
- **Missing the ICF framework:** Rehabilitation research should address activity and participation, not just body function impairment. LLMs may focus narrowly on impairment-level outcomes

## Methodology Decision Tree

```
What is the rehabilitation research question?
├── Does modality X improve outcome Y?
│   ├── Blinding possible? → RCT with sham control
│   ├── Blinding impossible? → Assessor-blind RCT, pragmatic design
│   └── Very rare condition? → Single-case experimental design (SCED)
├── What is the optimal dose/intensity?
│   ├── Known effective? → Dose-finding (factorial or dose-response) RCT
│   └── Unknown if effective? → Establish efficacy first, then optimize
├── Is program A equivalent to program B?
│   ├── Telerehabilitation vs in-person? → Non-inferiority RCT
│   └── Two active programs? → Equivalence or superiority RCT
├── How should a new service be implemented?
│   ├── Single site? → Before-after with process evaluation
│   ├── Multiple sites? → Stepped-wedge cluster RCT
│   └── Complex intervention? → MRC framework: development → feasibility → evaluation
├── What do patients experience?
│   ├── Alongside trial? → Embedded qualitative study (mixed methods)
│   └── Exploratory? → Qualitative study (phenomenology, grounded theory)
└── What outcomes should we measure?
    ├── Core outcome set? → Delphi consensus (COMET methodology)
    └── Validate existing measure? → COSMIN methodology
```
